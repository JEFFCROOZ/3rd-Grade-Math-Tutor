# 01 — Common Core Topics Reference

> What each topic covers, why it's taught this way, and what the NYS test actually asks.

---

## 3.OA — Operations & Algebraic Thinking

**The big idea:** Multiplication and division aren't just facts to memorize — they're relationships between equal groups.

**What's new vs. traditional:**
- **Arrays** — rows × columns as a physical model for multiplication. 3 rows of 4 = 3 × 4 = 12. This is the same structure as area (rows × columns = area), which is why 3rd grade connects multiplication and geometry.
- **Commutative property** — 3 × 4 = 4 × 3, shown visually by rotating the array 90°
- **Unknown factor** — "3 × ? = 12" before introducing the ÷ symbol. This builds the relationship between multiplication and division before formalizing it.

**Why parents find it confusing:** The traditional approach was "here are the facts, memorize them." Common Core deliberately delays memorization in favor of building understanding first. The facts come, but they're derived rather than imposed.

**NYS test focus:** Word problems with equal groups, arrays with missing dimensions, relating multiplication to division.

---

## 3.NBT — Number & Operations in Base Ten

**The big idea:** Place value is the foundation of all arithmetic. Every operation should be understood through the lens of what each digit represents.

**What's new vs. traditional:**
- **Rounding** uses the "halfway point" rule explicitly. Students look at the digit one place to the right and ask: is it closer to 0 or 100?
- **Addition and subtraction** are built on place value: add hundreds to hundreds, tens to tens, ones to ones. The "carrying" algorithm is taught as a shortcut for a place value concept, not a magic procedure.
- **Multiplying by 10** — 4 × 30 = 4 × 3 tens = 12 tens = 120. This pattern-based thinking prepares for multi-digit multiplication in 4th grade.

**Why parents find it confusing:** The traditional algorithm (stack, carry, done) is faster for people who already understand it. Common Core slows down to build the understanding first, which looks inefficient to parents but produces better long-term number sense.

**NYS test focus:** Rounding to nearest 10 and 100, adding/subtracting within 1,000, multiplying one-digit numbers by multiples of 10.

## 3.NF — Number & Operations — Fractions

**The big idea:** A fraction is a number, not just a "part of a pie." It has a specific location on the number line.

**What's new vs. traditional:**
- **Equal parts emphasis** — the denominator names the size of each part. 1/4 means "the whole is cut into 4 equal pieces."
- **Unit fractions first** — 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, 1/6, 1/8 are mastered before non-unit fractions. This builds intuition for what the denominator means.
- **Number line model** — fractions are placed on a number line from 0 to 1, making them real numbers rather than just ratios.
- **Comparing fractions** — using the number line model: 1/2 vs 1/4, same numerator means bigger denominator = smaller pieces.

**Why parents find it confusing:** Most adults learned fractions as "part of a circle." The number line model is unfamiliar but more mathematically powerful — it makes the transition to decimals and negative numbers much smoother later.

**NYS test focus:** Identifying fractions as parts of a whole, placing fractions on number lines, comparing fractions with same numerator or same denominator.

## 3.MD — Measurement & Data

**The big idea:** Measurement connects math to the physical world. Area connects back to multiplication (arrays).

**What's new vs. traditional:**
- **Area via tiling** — before using length × width, students fill a rectangle with unit squares and count. This makes the formula meaningful rather than arbitrary.
- **Area = multiplication** — a 4 × 6 rectangle has 4 rows of 6 unit squares = 4 × 6 = 24 square units. This is the same array model from 3.OA, making multiplication and area the same concept.
- **Telling time to the nearest minute** — more precise than the traditional nearest 5 minutes. Uses elapsed time strategies ("count up to the next hour, then add the rest").
- **Picture graphs and bar graphs** — reading scales, answering comparison questions ("how many more?")

**Why parents find it confusing:** Area being taught *through* multiplication (rather than as a separate geometry topic) surprises parents. It's intentional — showing that the same mathematical structure applies across contexts.

**NYS test focus:** Area of rectangles, perimeter, telling time, reading graphs including scaled bar graphs.

---

## 3.G — Geometry

**The big idea:** Shapes have attributes that define them. Partitioning shapes into equal parts connects geometry back to fractions.

**What's new vs. traditional:**
- **Attribute-based classification** — a square IS a rectangle (it has all the attributes of a rectangle, plus equal sides). Traditional teaching treated these as separate categories.
- **Partitioning into equal areas** — dividing a shape into halves, thirds, fourths. Each part is a fraction of the whole. This closes the loop with 3.NF.

**Why parents find it confusing:** "Is a square a rectangle?" Yes, in Common Core. This contradicts what many parents were taught (incorrectly) as children.

**NYS test focus:** Identifying shapes by attributes, understanding that different shapes can be divided into the same fractions, recognizing equal-area partitions.
